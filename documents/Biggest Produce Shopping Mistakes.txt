Chefs Say These Are the Biggest Mistakes People Make When Buying Produce
Shop like a chef to save money and enjoy the best fruits and vegetables.
By Merlyn Miller | Published on June 2, 2026
Key Points: 
* As grocery prices rise, we're always looking for ways to shop smarter, so we asked chefs to share the common mistakes they see people make when buying produce.
* Professional chefs highlighted six major mistakes in the produce department that contribute to food waste, higher checkout bills, and the purchase of produce that won't be high quality.
* If you want to save money and enjoy fruits and vegetables at their peak, avoid these errors.
We’re all familiar with the consequences of poor produce shopping. A box of greens that turns slimy because you bought it on impulse. A tomato or peach that’s so mealy inside it’s inedible. Or perhaps worst of all, racking up a shockingly high bill at checkout when all you wanted was to make a salad.
Making smart decisions when buying fruits and vegetables is key to saving money on groceries and to bringing home high-quality ingredients. When it comes to food shopping strategies, few people are more knowledgeable — or more adept — than chefs.
We asked professional chefs to share the most common mistakes they witness people make when buying fresh produce. From the errors that could be costing you money to the blunders that often result in food waste, these are the six tips they want you to know.
Buying pre-cut vegetables
Most grocery stores have a wall of prepared produce options, such as already-diced mirepoix, sliced stir-fry vegetables, and spiralized zucchini. When you’re pressed for time, the convenience of these products is obvious. However, Bill Peet, executive chef of Tavern On the Green in New York City’s Central Park, warns that "One mistake I see all the time is people buying pre-cut or pre-prepared vegetables for convenience. They may look nice, but most of the time they’re much more expensive, sometimes three or four times the price, and they’re not as fresh or ripe as whole produce."
Underestimating what you’ll find at the farmers market
It’s a common misconception that farmers markets are inherently expensive, and as grocery prices rise, many consumers may be avoiding them to save money. But 2018 Food & Wine Best New Chef and the co-owner and chef of Take Root Hospitality Michael Gallina says that farmers markets can surprise you. 
Per Gallina, “One mistake people make is overlooking farmers markets, assuming they’re more expensive when, in many cases, they [provide] comparable or even better value. I’ve been surprised recently, both at restaurants and at home, at how much prices at major retailers have increased, while many local farmers and smaller producers have kept pricing more stable on staples like eggs, meats, and produce.”
Not paying attention to when produce will be ripe
Let’s say you buy eight peak-season peaches, juicy, perfectly ripe, and ready to enjoy. The first two or three will be great, but after six or seven days you might notice slimy brown spots as the fruit becomes overripe. The chef and owner of Frita Batidos, Eve Aronoff, recommends keeping in mind “the difference between produce that continues to ripen after it is picked/purchased and produce that does not continue to ripen and simply deteriorates.” 
“Some examples of produce that will continue to ripen and get sweeter over time are bananas, peaches, plums, tomatoes, avocados, and cantaloupe… Some examples of produce that deteriorates include strawberries, pineapple, grapes, and cherries… Keeping this in mind can help you choose the desired ripeness level when you are shopping, which will allow you to enjoy the produce at its best and keep waste to a minimum!”
Sticking too closely to a set grocery list
When speaking with chefs about how they shop for produce, multiple experts emphasized the importance of flexibility. Chef Patrick Rebholz, vice president of culinary and operations at Yardbird Southern Kitchen + Bar, explains that “one of the biggest mistakes I see is people shopping without flexibility or seasonality in mind. Guests often walk into a grocery store with a fixed recipe list instead of buying what’s actually in peak season or on sale. Seasonal produce is almost always fresher, tastes better, and is more affordable because supply is naturally higher."
Ethan Stowell, 2008 F&W Best New Chef and the founder and chief executive officer of Ethan Stowell Restaurants, agrees, noting that “I don’t predetermine what we’re going to eat. I’ll think about what vegetables and sides are in season and pick the protein in the moment. So many grocery stores have items that are on sale. You can save a lot of money by not being super strict on your meal before you get to the store.”
Buying more than you can cook
Especially for households with just one or two people, it’s easy to purchase more produce than you can eat. Victor Vasconcellos, executive chef at Berimbau Brazilian Table in New York, notes that “One mistake I see a lot is people buying more produce than they’ll actually use. I always recommend shopping more realistically and thinking about what you truly cook during the week. It helps reduce waste and saves money.”
That advice is even more important when you’re buying fruits and vegetables that may have a shorter shelf life than you expect. According to Luis Fayad, the executive chef of jm Curley, Bogie’s Place, and The Wig Shop in Boston, “'Manager's Specials’ on produce are a great way to extend your family's food budget, but be careful not to go overboard. Produce goes on sale when it is reaching the end of its shelf life, which means it will go bad faster in your fridge. Make sure you don't buy more than you can use within the next few days, or you'll be literally throwing away your money.”
Not caring properly for the produce you buy
Smart shopping ends with how you store produce in the fridge. If you want your fruits and vegetables to last longer — saving money on each item you avoid wasting — you shouldn’t just toss everything straight into the crisper drawer. Kelly Courtney, 2001 F&W Best New Chef, says "the challenge most consumers face is a lack of knowledge of how to care for the produce they purchase.”
Kelly points out that berries, an expensive ingredient that spoils quickly, are a prime example of this — most shoppers store them in the plastic containers they buy them in. Instead, the chef recommends, “When you bring berries home, immediately remove them from their clamshell containers. You wash strawberries, blueberries, and blackberries, but not raspberries, as they are extremely delicate and will never recover unless you eat them immediately. Those go on a plate, [laid] flat on paper towels or parchment paper, and into refrigeration.”
The chef also recommends air-drying your blueberries and blackberries after washing them, then storing the fruit in a single layer in the fridge. It might sound tedious, but the result is berries that stay plump and fresh all week — and money that stays in your pocket.
