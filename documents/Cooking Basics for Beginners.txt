Cooking Basics for Beginners: Learn How to Cook
Updated on 27/02/2025 by Yovelina Minkova
If you want to learn how to cook, the first step is understanding the basics. Many beginners think that memorizing recipes will teach them how to cook, but that’s not the case. Recipes give instructions for specific dishes, but they don’t explain why certain ingredients behave the way they do or how different cooking methods affect food. Without this knowledge, you’ll struggle to cook without following step-by-step directions.
This guide focuses on core cooking techniques rather than individual recipes. You’ll learn how to handle ingredients, control heat, and use different cooking methods. By understanding these basics, you’ll be able to cook confidently, adjust dishes to your taste, and fix mistakes when they happen.

Essential kitchen tools every beginner cook needs
Before you start cooking, you need the right tools. You don’t need a fully stocked kitchen, but having a few essential items will make cooking easier and more efficient.
Knives and cutting boards
* Chef’s knife. The most versatile knife. Use it for chopping, slicing, and dicing.
* Paring knife. Useful for peeling and cutting small ingredients.
* Serrated knife. Best for slicing bread and soft foods like tomatoes.
A wood or plastic cutting board is necessary for a safe chopping surface. Avoid glass or stone boards because they dull knives quickly. Always clean your board properly to prevent bacteria buildup.
Pots and pans
You only need a few basic cookware pieces to start:
* Sauté pan (frying pan). Good for frying, searing, and sautéing. Stainless steel or cast iron is best.
* Saucepan. Useful for boiling pasta, making sauces, or cooking small portions.
* Stockpot. Needed for making soups, stews, or boiling large amounts of water.
* Baking sheet. Used for roasting vegetables, baking cookies, or cooking meats.
If you choose a non-stick pan, use wooden or silicone utensils to prevent scratches.
Measuring tools and mixing bowls
Cooking requires precise measurements. These tools will help:
* Measuring cups. One set is for dry ingredients and another for liquids.
* Measuring spoons. Needed for small amounts of ingredients like salt and spices.
* Kitchen scale. Helps measure ingredients by weight, which is more accurate than volume.
* Mixing bowls. Used for mixing ingredients, marinating food, or serving.
Utensils and other essentials
* Tongs. Helps flip food safely without piercing it.
* Spatula. Needed for flipping, stirring, or scraping food from pans.
* Whisk. Useful for mixing liquids and incorporating air into eggs or batters.
* Colander. Drains pasta, rinses vegetables, and strains liquids.
* Can opener. Required for opening canned foods.
What to avoid
* Cheap knives. They dull quickly and make cutting unsafe. Invest in one good chef’s knife instead of a full set of low-quality knives.
* Too many gadgets. Avoid tools that have only one purpose, like egg slicers or garlic presses. They take up space and don’t improve your cooking skills.
With these basic tools, you can prepare most dishes without difficulty. Next, let’s look at how heat affects cooking and why controlling it is important.
Learning about the heat and cooking techniques
When you cook food, you’re controlling how heat changes it. Understanding how heat works with different cooking methods will help you make better decisions in the kitchen and improve your cooking results.

Moist-heat cooking methods
When you cook with water or steam, you’re using moist heat. These methods work well for foods that need gentle cooking to become tender.
Boiling means cooking your food in water that has reached 212°F (100°C). When you see large bubbles rising steadily to the surface, your water is boiling. You’ll use this method for cooking pasta – the rapidly moving water prevents noodles from sticking together. However, boiling can be too harsh for delicate foods, as the bubbling water can break them apart.
Simmering is gentler than boiling – the water temperature stays between 180-200°F (82.2-93.3°C), with only small bubbles appearing. This method works perfectly for making soups and cooking tough cuts of meat until they become tender. You’ll know you’ve reached a simmer when you see small bubbles occasionally breaking the surface.
When you steam food, you’re cooking it with hot vapor instead of putting it directly in water. Place your food in a steamer basket above boiling water and cover the pot. Steaming preserves more nutrients than boiling because the food doesn’t sit in water. This makes it especially good for vegetables – they’ll stay bright and crisp-tender.
Poaching is the gentlest moist-heat method, using water between 160-180°F (71.1-82.2°C). You’ll hardly see any bubbles. This technique is perfect for eggs and delicate fish that could fall apart with rougher cooking. The water should be hot enough that you see tiny bubbles occasionally rising to the surface.

Dry-heat cooking methods
These techniques use heat without adding water. They create different textures and flavors than moist-heat methods.
When you sauté, you’re cooking food quickly in a small amount of hot oil. The pan should be hot enough that food sizzles when it touches the surface. Keep the food moving by stirring or tossing it in the pan. This prevents burning and ensures even cooking. Sautéing works great for cooking vegetables while keeping them crisp, and for browning meat quickly.
Pan-frying uses more oil than sautéing, but the food isn’t completely covered. The oil should be about 1/4 inch deep. This method creates a crispy outside while keeping the inside moist – perfect for breaded chicken cutlets or fish fillets. The oil should be hot enough that a small piece of bread turns golden brown in about 60 seconds.
Deep-frying means completely submerging food in hot oil, usually between 350-375°F (176.6-190.5°C). The oil needs to be hot enough that the food doesn’t become greasy – when it’s the right temperature, the food will bubble steadily but not violently when added. This method creates the crispy coating on french fries and fried chicken.
Roasting and baking both use the dry heat of your oven, but they’re slightly different. Roasting typically uses higher temperatures (400°F/204.4°C and up) to create browned, crispy exteriors on meats and vegetables. Baking uses moderate temperatures (350-375°F) for foods that need to cook through evenly, like breads and cakes.
Controlling Heat for Better Results
The temperature you choose matters as much as the cooking method. High heat (above 400°F) creates browning and crispy textures, but can quickly burn food if you’re not careful. Lower temperatures (below 350°F) cook food more slowly and evenly, which helps prevent burning and drying out.
When you’re cooking meat, always let it rest after cooking. The time depends on size – 5-10 minutes for a chicken breast, 15-20 for a large roast. This allows the meat’s internal temperature to even out and keeps the juices from running out when you cut it.
Your choice of cooking equipment affects how well you can control heat. Heavy pots and pans distribute heat more evenly than thin ones. A pan that’s too crowded will steam food instead of browning it because the moisture can’t evaporate quickly enough. Give food enough space – you should be able to see the pan’s bottom between pieces.

Building your knife skills
Good knife skills make cooking faster, safer, and more enjoyable. Knowing how to handle a knife properly helps you cut ingredients evenly, prevent accidents, and improve cooking consistency. Before you start chopping, you need the right tools and techniques.
You don’t need a full knife set. Three essential knives cover most kitchen tasks:
* Chef’s knife (8-10 inches). The most versatile knife for slicing, dicing, and chopping vegetables, meat, and herbs.
* Paring knife (3-4 inches). Best for peeling, trimming, and small cutting tasks like mincing garlic.
* Serrated knife. Ideal for slicing bread, tomatoes, and other soft foods without crushing them.
A sharp knife is safer than a dull one because it requires less force and reduces the risk of slipping. Keep your knives sharp with a honing steel and sharpen them regularly with a whetstone or professional service.
How to hold a knife properly
A secure grip gives you control and prevents accidents. Use the pinch grip:
1. Pinch the blade with your thumb and index finger where it meets the handle.
2. Wrap your other fingers around the handle for stability.
3. Keep your wrist relaxed to make cutting movements smoother.
Your non-cutting hand should use the “claw grip” to hold ingredients safely:
* Tuck your fingertips in and press them against the ingredient.
* Use your knuckles as a guide for the knife.
* Move your hand back as you cut, keeping the blade against your knuckles.
Essential knife cuts you need to learn
When you’re cooking, the way you cut your ingredients directly affects how they cook. Cutting food into pieces of the same size ensures everything cooks at the same speed, helping you avoid having some pieces overcooked while others remain raw.
Let’s explore the most important ways to cut ingredients and how to do each one properly:
Slicing
When you need to cut larger ingredients into thinner pieces, slicing should be your choice. You’ll use it often for onions, peppers, and meats. To slice effectively, hold your knife firmly and use a smooth forward motion while rocking the blade. The key is to let the weight of the knife do most of the work – you don’t need to press down hard.
Dicing
You’ll use dicing when you need uniform cubes, which is especially important for vegetables that take longer to cook, like carrots and potatoes. To dice properly, first cut your ingredients into evenly-sized slices. Then cut these slices into strips of equal width. Finally, cut across these strips to create cubes. When a recipe calls for a “medium dice,” aim for pieces about 1/2 inch across. A “small dice” should be about 1/4 inch, while a “large dice” is closer to 3/4 inch.
Julienne Cutting
This technique creates thin, matchstick-shaped pieces that cook quickly and look appealing in dishes like stir-fries. To julienne a vegetable, first cut it into thin slabs about 1/8 inch thick. Stack these slabs and cut them lengthwise into strips about 1/8 inch wide. For most vegetables, your julienne strips should be about 2-3 inches long.
Mincing
When you need very finely chopped ingredients, like garlic or herbs for a sauce, mincing is the technique to use. Start by roughly chopping your ingredients. Then, keeping the tip of your knife on the cutting board, rock the blade back and forth over the food, moving across it systematically. Keep mincing until the pieces are very fine – for garlic, they should be almost paste-like.
Chiffonade
This cut creates thin ribbons from leafy ingredients like basil or spinach. Stack several leaves on top of each other, then roll them tightly like a cigar. Use your knife to cut across the roll, creating thin strips. This technique prevents herbs from bruising and creates an attractive garnish for your dishes.
Remember that consistent cutting takes practice. Start slowly and focus on making your cuts even rather than fast. Speed will come naturally as you become more comfortable with each technique.

How to season food and develop flavor
Seasoning is one of the most important skills in cooking. It’s not just about adding salt. Balancing flavors correctly can transform a simple dish into something delicious. Understanding how different seasonings work will help you make food that tastes better and more complex.
Salt is the most essential seasoning because it enhances natural flavors. When used correctly, it brings out the taste of ingredients without making food taste salty.
When to add salt:
* Before cooking. Helps season meats, vegetables, and pasta water from the inside out.
* During cooking. Balances flavors as they develop.
* After cooking. Adjusts seasoning at the end for a final flavor boost.
Culinary tip from our instructors: when cooking pasta, add enough salt to the water so it tastes slightly salty. This ensures the pasta is seasoned from within, rather than just on the surface.
So salt is a big must in your cooking, however, you should always keep in mind that you shouldn’t overdo it. Adding too much salt at once is quite a typical mistake beginners make. To avoid it, always start with a small amount and taste as you go.
How does acidity, sweetness, bitterness, and umami work
Salt isn’t the only way to enhance flavor. The best dishes have a balance of different flavor elements:
* Acidity (brightens flavors). Found in vinegar, lemon juice, tomatoes, and yogurt. Helps balance rich or fatty foods. Example: A squeeze of lemon on grilled fish enhances freshness.
* Sweetness (softens acidity and bitterness). Found in sugar, honey, fruits, and caramelized onions. Example: Adding a small amount of sugar to tomato sauce reduces acidity.
* Bitterness (adds depth). Found in coffee, dark chocolate, kale, and radicchio. Used to contrast richness. Example: A bitter green salad pairs well with a rich, creamy dressing.
* Umami (adds savory depth). Found in soy sauce, mushrooms, aged cheese, and meat. Example: Parmesan cheese enhances pasta dishes by adding umami.
If a dish tastes too bland, try adding a small amount of acid (lemon or vinegar) instead of more salt—it often enhances flavor without over-salting.
When and how to use herbs and spices
Herbs and spices add depth and complexity to food. Knowing when to add them can make a big difference in flavor.
Fresh vs. dried herbs:
* Fresh herbs (basil, cilantro, parsley) have delicate flavors. Add them at the end of cooking or as a garnish.
* Dried herbs (oregano, thyme, rosemary) have more concentrated flavors. Add them early in cooking so they have time to release their oils.
Common spice pairings:
* Cumin + coriander ? Works well in Middle Eastern and Indian dishes.
* Garlic + onion Powder ? A base seasoning for many savory dishes.
* Paprika + cayenne ? Adds smokiness and heat.
* Cinnamon + nutmeg ? Used in both sweet and savory dishes.
Culinary tip from our instructors: Toasting whole spices in a dry pan before using them enhances their flavor by releasing natural oils.
Common seasoning mistakes that can affect your cooking
One of the most important cooking skills is knowing how to season your food properly. Many home cooks struggle with seasoning, which can turn an otherwise well-cooked meal into something disappointing. Let’s explore the common mistakes and learn how to fix them.
Not using enough seasoning. When your food tastes flat or boring, it usually means you haven’t added enough seasoning. Salt is particularly important – it doesn’t just make food taste salty, it helps bring out the natural flavors of your ingredients. Start by adding a small amount of salt at the beginning of cooking. If your dish still feels like it’s missing something, try adding a splash of acid like lemon juice or vinegar. Acid helps balance flavors and adds brightness to your food.
Adding spices at the wrong time. Timing matters when you’re working with spices. When you add whole or ground spices too late in the cooking process, they won’t have time to release their flavors into the food. Instead, they’ll taste raw and harsh. Most spices need heat to develop their full flavor – this is why many recipes start by cooking spices in oil. As a general rule, add ground spices near the beginning of cooking, giving them at least 15-20 minutes to cook with your other ingredients.
Overwhelming your food with too many seasonings. Using too many different spices in one dish can create confused, muddled flavors where nothing stands out. Instead of reaching for every spice in your cabinet, focus on 2-3 that work well together. For example, if you’re making a simple curry, you might use just cumin, coriander, and turmeric. This approach lets each spice contribute its distinct flavor while creating a balanced final dish.
Remember that you can always add more seasoning, but you can’t take it away once it’s in your food. Start with a lighter hand and gradually add more until the flavors taste right to you. As you cook more, you’ll develop a better sense of how much seasoning different dishes need.

The next steps to improve your cooking skills
As you practice cooking more, you’ll naturally become more confident in the kitchen. However, there are still a few tips we want to give to you that will make you grow further. And one of them is to focus on one technique at a time. Instead of trying to learn everything at once, master one cooking method before moving to the next. Start with sautéing – practice controlling your heat and moving food around the pan until you can consistently achieve a golden brown color. Then try roasting, where you’ll learn how dry heat develops flavor in vegetables and meats. Finally, work on simmering to understand how to control temperature when cooking with liquids.
Always learn from your mistakes. Every cook makes mistakes, and knowing how to fix them is an important skill. If your food is too salty, adding acid or unsalted ingredients can help balance the flavor. For bland dishes, try increasing the seasoning gradually or adding contrasting flavors. If meat is overcooked, you can often save it by using it in a soup or sauce where additional moisture will help improve the texture.
As your skills improve, challenge yourself with increasingly complex tasks. Begin with basics like properly cooking eggs or sautéing vegetables. When you’re comfortable with these, move on to cooking complete meals using multiple techniques. Eventually, you’ll be able to adapt recipes based on available ingredients and cook confidently without following exact instructions.
